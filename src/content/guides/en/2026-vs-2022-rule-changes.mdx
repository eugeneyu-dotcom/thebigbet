Every World Cup cycle brings some amount of rule and technology updates. This time, moving from Qatar to North America means the scale and environment are completely different from last time, which has brought a fair number of substantive changes along with it. Here's a roundup of the ones worth knowing about.

## Tournament Format: 32 Teams Became 48, Which Changed the Entire Qualification Logic

The biggest change, obviously, is the field expanding from 32 to 48 teams, with the number of groups going from 8 to 12 and the total match count jumping from 64 to 104. The Round-of-32 qualification list now also includes an extra layer of "best third-placed teams" filtering — for the detailed logic, check out our earlier [complete breakdown of the qualification rules](/en/guides/2026-world-cup-qualification-rules); no need to repeat it here.

## Hosting Model: From One Country to Three Sharing the Load

Qatar 2022 was hosted by a single country, with every stadium just a short drive from the others. This time, the United States, Canada, and Mexico are co-hosting, with 16 stadiums spread across three countries and multiple time zones — pushing the travel distances for teams and fans into a completely different category. That said, since transportation across North America generally relies heavily on driving, a lot of the feedback so far has criticized inadequate public transit and long waits for shuttle buses — something worth keeping in mind if you're planning to attend in person.

## Stadium Turf: NFL Stadiums Get a Temporary "Makeover"

This is one of the more unusual behind-the-scenes engineering efforts this cycle. Eight of the US host venues are normally artificial-turf NFL stadiums; to meet World Cup pitch standards, all eight have had natural grass temporarily installed. All 16 stadiums ultimately settled on a "hybrid turf" system — 90 to 95 percent natural grass woven with 5 to 10 percent synthetic fiber, which boosts the pitch's durability and stability. The stadium in Dallas even had its playing surface raised by four feet just to fit a regulation-size soccer pitch.

## Hydration Breaks: From "Weather-Dependent" to "Mandatory"

At Qatar 2022, hydration breaks were called at the referee's discretion based on heat conditions on the day. This time, it's written directly into the rules: every match now has a mandatory three-minute hydration break at the 22-minute mark of both halves, regardless of weather — no longer a judgment call, but a standardized, enforced part of the game.

## Substitution Rules: Same Count, New Penalty for Time-Wasting

The number of permitted substitutions stays the same as last time — five per match, unchanged. But a new rule has been added: once the fourth official raises the substitution board, the player being replaced has only 10 seconds to leave the pitch, and dawdling or deliberately wasting time can now draw a penalty. Separately, the goalkeeper's time limit for holding the ball has been extended from 6 to 8 seconds, with a corner awarded to the opponent if exceeded.

There are also two often-overlooked "special sixth substitution" scenarios worth knowing about: first, if a match goes to extra time, both teams get one additional substitution; second, if a player is suspected of having a concussion, a team can use an extra "concussion substitute" that doesn't count against the normal substitution limit — and if one team uses this, the opposing team automatically receives one additional regular substitution as a balancing measure.

## Offside Technology Upgrade: From "After-the-Fact Review" to "Instant Audio Alert"

Semi-automated offside technology (SAOT) actually debuted back at Qatar 2022, but it's been upgraded further this time: the moment a clear offside is detected, the system sends an instant audio alert directly to the officials, removing the need to wait on a slow video review. FIFA has also built a dedicated 3D body model for every one of the 1,248 players across all 48 squads this cycle, used to precisely calculate limb positioning for offside calls.

## Expanded VAR Intervention

VAR's scope of intervention has also been widened, now covering incorrectly awarded second yellow cards (and the resulting red card), mistaken-identity calls on the wrong player, and goals that directly resulted from an incorrectly awarded corner kick — all of which can now be corrected through VAR review.

## A Word From the Author

Taken together, the direction of this cycle's changes is pretty consistent: rules that used to be flexible and "situation-dependent" have gradually become explicit, mandatory standard procedures — hydration breaks, substitution time-wasting penalties, and offside calls all follow the same logic. For fans, this should make match pacing more predictable; but for teams, these seemingly minor procedural details could end up deciding a match at a critical moment somewhere down the line — case in point, several teams have already pushed back on the mandatory hydration break, arguing that it's unnecessary in air-conditioned venues and disrupts the flow of the game.
